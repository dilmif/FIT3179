World Happiness

This dashboard provides a comprehensive view into World Happiness in 2023. Explore happiness scores, continent comparisons, top-ranking countries, and factors influencing well-being. Dive into the global happiness landscape.

World Happiness in 2023

This choropleth map represents happiness scores using a gradient of green shades, with darker green indicating higher scores and lighter green representing lower scores. Hover over any country to view its happiness score.

Nordic European countries such as Finland, Norway, and Sweden appear in dark green on the map, signifying that they have some of the highest happiness scores globally.

In contrast, African and Asian countries, such as Afghanistan and Sierra Leone, tend to appear in lighter green on the map, indicating lower happiness scores. These regions often face challenges related to economic hardship, political instability, and lower access to basic services, all of which can impact happiness and life satisfaction.

World Happiness per Continent

This box plot showcases global happiness disparities and diverse well-being levels across continents.

Europe stands out with the highest median happiness score and maximum score, closely followed by North America and South America. The data of these continents is tightly clustered with minimal spread, reflecting its high overall well-being. In contrast, Asia and Oceania exhibit more variation, exemplified by outliers. Africa presents the lowest median happiness score and the widest spread, emphasizing the challenges faced by many African nations in achieving well-being.

Ranking of the Happiest Countries

This bump chart represents the top 10 countries with the highest happiness scores from 2015 through to 2023.

Seven of these countries are European, as indicated in different shades of green. Most of these countries are Nordic countries, known for their high living standards and quality of life.

New Zealand and Australia have satyed consistent within their rankings, with Canada dropping down four places from 2015 to 2023. Each of these contries have had a happiness score of over 6.9 throughout the years.

World Happiness vs GDP Per Capita

This bubble chart highlights the correlation of higher GDP per Capita with higher happiness score. Each circle represents a country, with a colour matching their continent and a bubble size representing its population.

Overall, European countries, represented by green bubbles, are in the top right corner indicating high GDP per Capita and high happiness score. Luxembourg is shown to have the highest GDP per Capita, and Finland is shown to have the highest happiness score.

In the bottom left corner, there are primarily more African countries, indicated by red bubbles. Sierra Leone is seen to have the lowest GDP per Capita, and Afghanistan is seen to have the lowest happiness Score.

Hover over any bubble to see information about the country, its continent, happiness score, GDP per Capita and population. Select a continent you want to see in particular, and drag the slider to show countries with a population greater than what you have chosen.

Various Key Indicators Related to Happiness

These small multiple bar charts represent indicators related to happiness. These factors include GDP per Capita, freedom, social support, generosity and life expectancy. The scores for these categories were calculated using survey data.

Notably, Europe scores highly in each category, having the highest mean value for GDP per Capita, social support and life expectancy. Conversely, North America and South America share similarities in their scores, especially when it comes to GDP per capita, social support, life expectancy and generosity.

However, both continents record relatively lower generosity scores, highlighting an individualistic community. On the other hand, Africa, Asia and Oceania score highly in generosity. Asia scoring the highest underscores a profound sense of community support and sharing. Africa, while scoring the lowest in all other components, has a high score in generosity. This illustrates the strong collectivistic culture, compared to the individualistic culture in North and South America.
